Act. 01 / 03 Regulatory & Design Frameworks FDA · mHealth · Dexcom CGM · Wearable Limitations

Activities covering the regulatory landscape and design challenges of AI-powered wearable medical devices — an essential context for understanding what it takes to deploy AI in clinical settings.

Activity 01
FDA and mHealth Regulation
FDA's Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) framework. Discussion of additional oversight beyond FDA: third-party algorithmic audits, ethical review boards for bias, international harmonisation.
Activity 03
Dexcom CGM Design Analysis
Multi-level design analysis of the Dexcom Continuous Glucose Monitor across mechanical (waterproof transmitter, applicator), software/app (color-coded glucose display, trend arrows), and system (Bluetooth + cloud sync) levels.
Activity 04
Activity Monitor Limitations in Obesity
Hip-worn monitors show decreased accuracy due to altered gait and postural control. Wrist-worn affected by placement at higher BMI. Proposed: combined multi-site sensing with subject-specific calibration models.
Act. 02 / 05 / 06 Hardware & Sensor Activities Seeed XIAO nRF52840 · Accelerometer · PPG · Gesture

Hands-on work with real wearable hardware — the Seeed Studio XIAO nRF52840 Sense (BLE-enabled microcontroller with IMU) — collecting accelerometer and PPG sensor data across different body placements and activity conditions.

Activity 5 — Accelerometer data collection (50 Hz)

Three activity types collected in-residence: Sitting (stationary on bed), Walking (regular-pace), Stairs (ascending and descending). Confusion matrix and classification report included for on-device activity recognition model.

Activity 6 — Gesture recognition

Acceleration plots for Flex gesture (wrist flexion) and Punch gesture — showing distinct temporal patterns enabling classification from raw accelerometer data.

Embedding × threshold grid search (Activity 11)

Instead of manual hyperparameter tuning, a systematic grid search over 230 combinations: embedding size [8, 10, 12, 14, 16, 18, 20, 24, 28, 32] × threshold [0.018→0.040, step 0.001]. Demonstrates principled search methodology for lite edge models.

MIT App Inventor (Activity 9)

Mobile application built using MIT App Inventor — deployed on iPhone. Designer section (UI layout) and Blocks section (event-driven logic) both documented.

Act. 15 MIMIC-IV ICU Clinical Data Analysis MIMIC-IV · ICU Demographics · Admission Types · Clinical AI

Analysis of MIMIC-IV — a real-world clinical dataset covering ICU admissions across unit types and patient demographics. This activity sits at the intersection of this course and my medical imaging AI focus — understanding clinical data structure is prerequisite to deploying AI in clinical settings.

Key findings

MICU (Medical ICU) admits the highest number of older patients (>89 years) with over 50,000 patients. Emergency admission type is the most common with 42,071 admissions. Visualisations of patient demographics, admission types, and ICU unit distribution reveal the clinical data landscape that AI systems must navigate.

★ What this course taught — uniquely

The full stack of clinical AI deployment — from regulation to hardware to model to data. No other course in this portfolio covered all of: FDA regulatory frameworks, embedded microcontroller programming, data collection from real sensors, lite model architectures for battery-constrained hardware, grid-based hyperparameter search, mobile app deployment, and real clinical dataset analysis (MIMIC-IV). The breadth is the point — deploying AI in healthcare requires understanding every layer of this stack simultaneously.

Edge AI as a real hardware constraint, not just a benchmark. The Seeed Studio XIAO nRF52840 activity (collecting accelerometer data at 50 Hz from three activity types) made the gap between a laptop GPU and an edge microcontroller tangible. Model choices that are trivial on cloud compute become tightly constrained when battery life, memory, and inference latency are physical limits. This constraint-first thinking directly informed my understanding of the WECARE project's 0.033ms latency target.

MIMIC-IV as a clinical data structure reference. Working with a real de-identified ICU dataset — understanding its schema, admission type distribution, and patient age distribution — gave context for what production clinical datasets actually look like. This is the bridge between building models on clean benchmarks and deploying them in real healthcare environments where data is messy, incomplete, and clinically nuanced.

YOLOv8 end-to-end — from phone camera to deployed model. Collecting 186 food images via smartphone, training YOLOv8, and achieving mAP@50 of 0.913 connected the full pipeline that most courses only teach in pieces. Understanding the annotation → training → evaluation → deployment loop from personal experience (including the data collection step, not just the training step) is directly transferable to any medical imaging annotation and training workflow.
