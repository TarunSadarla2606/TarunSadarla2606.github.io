Overview

EDA and RFM analysis on a distributed compute platform

Building the analytical foundation for a team's clustering pipeline — entirely on AWS at scale.

This project analyses the UCI Online Retail dataset — 541,909 transaction records across 38 countries from a UK-based online retailer. The goal is to segment customers using RFM analysis to identify patterns that feed into downstream K-Means and Bisecting K-Means clustering.

The entire pipeline runs on AWS — raw data on S3, computation on EMR Spark clusters, outputs saved back to S3. My specific contribution was the complete 60-cell EDA and the RFM feature engineering pipeline, whose output drove Ramyasri's clustering stage.

Most striking finding: A single customer (ID 16547) accounts for £1,772,220 — roughly 18% of the entire revenue from 541,909 transactions. This kind of concentration makes standard clustering approaches fragile without outlier handling, which directly informed the team's preprocessing decisions.

RFM Analysis

Building the segmentation features

R
Recency
Days since customer's last purchase, relative to the dataset's maximum date. Lower = more recent = higher value customer.
F
Frequency
Count of unique invoice numbers per customer. High frequency = loyal, engaged customer relationship.
M
Monetary
Total revenue per customer (Quantity × UnitPrice, rounded to 2dp). Identifies high-value vs low-value segments.
# RFM computation in PySpark on AWS EMR
recency_df = df.groupBy("CustomerID").agg(max("InvoiceTimestamp")...)
            .withColumn("Recency", datediff(lit(max_date), col("LastPurchaseDate")))
frequency_df = df.groupBy("CustomerID").agg(countDistinct("InvoiceNo").alias("Frequency"))
monetary_df = df.groupBy("CustomerID").agg(round(sum("Revenue"), 2).alias("Monetary"))
rfm_df = recency_df.join(frequency_df, "CustomerID").join(monetary_df, "CustomerID")

★ What this project taught — uniquely

Distributed computing on real cloud infrastructure. Every other project in this portfolio runs on a single machine — a laptop, Kaggle GPU, or cloud notebook. This was the first time I ran actual Spark jobs on an AWS EMR cluster with multiple EC2 nodes, with data stored on S3 rather than locally. Understanding how Spark's lazy evaluation, partitioning, and shuffle operations behave differently at scale — versus on a single-node PySpark installation — was a qualitatively different experience from working with pandas on a DataFrame.

RFM as a structured approach to customer segmentation. RFM is a decades-old framework that survived because it captures something real: when, how often, and how much a customer buys. Building it from scratch in PySpark (groupBy + agg + join chains) made the data engineering behind what looks like a simple concept tangible. Getting the recency computation right — relative to the dataset's max date, not a fixed reference — required careful thinking about what "recency" actually means in a historical dataset.

Outlier detection as a business question, not a statistical one. When Customer 16547 accounts for 18% of all revenue, the question of whether to include or exclude them isn't a statistical decision — it's a business decision about whether the model should describe typical customers or all customers. This framing of data decisions as business questions (not just technical ones) is directly transferable to any clinical AI project where data quality decisions have real-world consequences.
