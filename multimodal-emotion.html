Overview

Reading emotion without words

Visual and acoustic signals alone — no transcription, no text.

This project explores multimodal emotion recognition using only visual (facial expression from video frames) and acoustic (audio features from speech) cues, without any text input. The dataset is MELD — conversations from the Friends TV series with 7-class emotion labels and 3-class sentiment labels — which presents real-world challenges: heavy class imbalance (Neutral dominates at ~47%), very short clips, and subtle emotional expression.

I served as project lead for the 4-person team and owned the complete visual stream — from raw frame extraction through to the final dual-head prediction. The audio stream (BiLSTM) and the multimodal fusion module were handled by my teammates.

The fusion problem: Multimodal fusion did not improve over individual streams. This turned out to be the most instructive finding — simple feature concatenation without temporal alignment of video and audio fails on real conversational data. One-frame-per-3-seconds is a fundamentally lossy video representation. Attention-based fusion with proper temporal modeling is the clear next step.

Dataset

MELD — Friends TV conversational emotion data

9,989 training / 1,112 validation / 4,807 test utterances from the Friends TV series. Each utterance is a short clip with emotion and sentiment labels. Key challenge: Neutral dominates at ~47% of training data — Fear and Disgust each have fewer than 300 training samples. This class imbalance heavily impacts recall on minority emotions throughout all models.

Architecture

Visual stream — two models, one fusion branch

Baseline — my work
Custom 5-Layer CNN
5-layer convolutional network built from scratch with Leaky ReLU, batch normalization, and max pooling. Dual output heads for simultaneous sentiment and emotion prediction. Serves as the performance baseline for the visual stream.
Improved — my work
Pretrained VGG-16
VGG-16 (ImageNet pretrained) with classification head replaced by two task-specific fully connected heads. Transfer learning provides rich hierarchical visual features. Improved visual accuracy by ~8% over CNN baseline across both tasks.

Video feature branch for multimodal fusion

# VGG-16 backbone → adaptive average pooling → FC → 100-D video embedding
VGG-16 features → AdaptiveAvgPool → FC(100) → video_feature_vector

# Audio branch (teammate's work — BiLSTM)
Audio features → BiLSTM × 2 → audio_feature_vector (192-D)

# Fusion
concat([video_100D, audio_192D]) → FC → [Sentiment head | Emotion head]

Future Work

What the fusion failure points toward

Temporal video modeling — sampling 1 frame per 3 seconds discards most of the dynamic information in facial expression. Proper temporal CNNs or video transformers processing multiple consecutive frames are the obvious next step.

Attention-based fusion — instead of simple concatenation, cross-modal attention would let the model learn which modality is more informative for each specific utterance. Critical for robustness to noisy audio or obscured faces.

Class-balancing strategies — oversampling, class-weighted loss, or generating synthetic examples for Disgust and Fear (each ~270 training samples) to address the zero-recall problem on minority emotions.

Facial detection preprocessing — cropping to detected face regions before feeding to the visual model should substantially improve accuracy on cluttered or multi-person frames.

★ What this project taught — uniquely

Why simple fusion fails on real conversational data. The multimodal fusion did not improve over individual streams — and understanding exactly why is more valuable than if it had worked. Simple feature concatenation (100-D video + 192-D audio) without temporal alignment means the model sees a video feature vector computed from 1 frame per 3 seconds alongside audio features computed from the full clip. Those two representations describe different windows of the same utterance. Learning to diagnose a fusion architecture failure — and articulate specifically what would fix it (attention-based cross-modal alignment, temporal video modeling) — is a skill that applies to any multimodal clinical pipeline.

Multi-task learning with dual output heads. Every other classification project in this portfolio has a single output. This was the first project with two simultaneous targets — sentiment (3 classes) and emotion (7 classes) — sharing a backbone but with separate heads. Understanding how multi-task objectives interact during training (when they help each other, when they compete) and why a single forward pass can produce two predictions is directly applicable to clinical AI systems that must simultaneously predict multiple diagnoses or measurements from the same scan.

Transfer learning for a task far from ImageNet. VGG-16 was pretrained on ImageNet (1,000-class natural image classification) and fine-tuned for facial emotion recognition — a task with very different discriminative features (subtle muscle movements vs object outlines). Observing that fine-tuning improved over the from-scratch CNN by ~8% despite the domain gap demonstrated something concrete: hierarchical visual representations are partially transferable even across very different tasks, as long as the early layers' edge/texture detectors remain useful.
