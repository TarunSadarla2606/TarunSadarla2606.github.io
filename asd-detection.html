Origin

The project that made me decide

Why this one is different from everything else in this portfolio.

Every other project in this portfolio was built during my MS — deliberate, structured, informed by two years of deep learning knowledge I'd accumulated with a specific goal in mind. 🌐 For everyone

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is a neurological condition affecting how people communicate, interact, and perceive the world around them. Diagnosis today is done through behavioural assessments — observing a child over time, which is slow, subjective, and often delayed by years. The earlier it's detected, the earlier support can begin. That matters enormously.

My project was built on a simple question: can a deep learning model look at an MRI scan of a brain and detect structural differences that correlate with ASD — differences that exist in the brain's anatomy but are invisible to the naked eye?

⚙️ Technical context

Current diagnostic gold standard — the ADOS and ADI-R behavioural assessments — require experienced clinicians, take hours, and introduce significant inter-rater variability. Neuroimaging-based approaches offer a path toward objective, reproducible classification grounded in biological structure. The ABIDE-I dataset, released by the ABIDE Consortium, made this research possible by aggregating sMRI and phenotypic data from 17 sites worldwide.

Why this project became my career

The result wasn't a fluke — the model worked, and worked consistently across multiple architectures and optimizers. But more than the numbers, it was the nature of the problem that stayed with me. Medical imaging AI sits at the intersection of everything that makes deep learning hard and everything that makes it matter. Small datasets, high stakes, interpretability requirements, deployment constraints. When I applied for my MS immediately after graduation, I already knew this was the field I wanted to spend it on.

Dataset

ABIDE-I: a multi-site neuroimaging dataset

1,112 subjects. 17 research centres. One shared goal.

🌐 For everyone

The ABIDE dataset is a landmark example of open science in neuroscience. Researchers from 17 universities and hospitals across the world — each using their own MRI machines and protocols — agreed to pool their brain scan data into a single shared resource. Without this kind of collaboration, no single site would have enough data to train a reliable model. I accessed the dataset through COINS and processed it from scratch.

⚙️ Technical detail

Starting from 1,112 subjects (564 ASD, 548 TC), I removed subjects with missing sMRI data or "Unknown" diagnostic labels — leaving 1,067 usable subjects. Brain volumes arrived as NIfTI (.nii.gz) files, which I converted to DICOM and then extracted as PNG slices. After removing empty and malformed slices, 100,510 usable slices remained for training.

Preprocessing pipelines

🌐 For everyone

Brain MRI scans are not just images — they're 3D volumes of brain tissue, stored in a specialized scientific format. Before any neural network can see them, they need to be converted into regular 2D images, cleaned, and standardized. I built two separate preprocessing pipelines: one for the CNN models, and a more complex one for the Vision Transformer experiments.

Models

Five architectures, two frameworks, one lesson

What I built, what worked, and what the ViT failure taught me about data scale.

Model 01

Custom 5-Layer CNN

Purpose-built convolutional network for binary ASD/TC classification. Each block: Conv → LeakyReLU → MaxPool(2×2) → Dropout(20%). Channels scale 16→32→64→128→256. Flatten → FC(100) → Output(2). Trained with Adam, NAdam, and RMSprop.

91% Test Acc · AUC 0.97

Model 02

Skip-Connected CNN

Same 5-layer architecture with a residual skip connection from layer 1 to layer 3. Addresses vanishing gradients and encourages feature reuse across depth. Marginal but consistent improvement over the plain CNN — particularly with NAdam optimizer.

91% Test Acc · AUC 0.98 (NAdam)

Model 03

Vision Transformer (Keras)

Custom ViT in TensorFlow/Keras from scratch. 6 Transformer blocks, 4 attention heads, 128 projection dimensions. MLP: 512→256 with GELU activation. 196 patches (16×16) per image. 100 epochs due to GPU constraints.

~60% Acc · Near Random

Model 04

Vision Transformer (PyTorch)

Fully custom ViT from scratch in PyTorch — no pretrained weights. PatchEncoder with Conv2d projection + learnable positional embeddings + CLS token. Multiple hyperparameter configurations explored across dedicated search notebooks.

Underperformed · Data insufficient

Model 05

Pretrained ViT (Post-submission)

After formal submission, fine-tuned vit_base_patch16_224 and vit_small_patch16_224 via timm, plus google/vit-base-patch16-224 via HuggingFace on paid GPU sessions. Trained weights not retained — setup and training loop documented in notebooks.

Exploratory · Weights not saved

How the architectures were trained

⚙️ Technical detail

CNN models were trained with three optimizers — Adam, NAdam, and RMSprop — each with LR=0.001, 50 epochs, batch size 64. Cross-entropy loss. Dropout at 20% per block for regularization. The optimizer comparison was deliberately systematic: all other hyperparameters held constant.

The custom ViT was trained with RMSprop at LR=0.001 for 100 epochs — twice as long as the CNNs — reflecting the known requirement of Vision Transformers for more training steps to converge. It wasn't enough.

The ViT lesson: Vision Transformers have no inductive biases — they don't assume that nearby pixels are related, unlike CNNs. They learn everything from data. That makes them powerful on massive datasets but completely data-hungry at this scale. 100,510 slices from 1,067 subjects is nowhere near enough for a ViT trained from scratch.

The pretrained ViT experiments (post-submission, on Paperspace GPU) were designed to test this directly — pretrained weights give the model the structural priors it needs. Consistent with expectations, fine-tuning outperformed training from scratch substantially.

Explainability — LIME (post-submission exploration)

🌐 For everyone

Knowing a model is accurate is not the same as knowing it's right for the right reasons. A model could theoretically learn to classify MRI scans by detecting scanner noise patterns rather than brain structure — and be accurate while being completely useless clinically. This is why explainability matters deeply in medical AI.

⚙️ Technical detail

After submission, I built a LIME explainability notebook. The approach: load a trained CNN from saved weights, pass an MRI slice through LimeImageExplainer.explain_instance(), and visualize which superpixel regions of the brain contributed most to the ASD/TC classification decision via mark_boundaries(). The setup and explainer invocation ran successfully — visualization results were incomplete due to an error before the final annotated image output. GradCAM was also explored but notebooks were not retained. Both remain open threads for future work.

ViT result is near-random classification (~60%). This is consistent with the known data hunger of Vision Transformers trained from scratch — not a model failure, but a dataset scale problem.

Why the ViT failed — and why that's an important finding: Vision Transformers have no inductive biases. CNNs assume local spatial structure — nearby pixels are related — which maps well onto brain anatomy. ViTs make no such assumption and must learn spatial relationships entirely from data. At this dataset scale (~100K slices from ~1,000 subjects), there simply isn't enough signal. The pretrained ViT experiments run after submission directly tested this hypothesis. This finding directly informed my research direction in subsequent projects.

🧠
CNNs dominate at this data scale
91% test accuracy and AUC 0.97–0.98 across all 6 CNN runs, with stable training curves and no overfitting. The inductive biases of convolution align naturally with neuroimaging structure.
🔗
Skip connections help — selectively
Skip connections improved AUC from 0.97 → 0.98 with NAdam and raised specificity across all optimizers. Marginal but consistent — gradient flow matters even for 5-layer networks.
📊
Optimizer choice matters less than architecture
Within each architecture, all three optimizers (Adam, NAdam, RMSprop) produced comparable results. Architecture selection has far larger impact on final performance than optimizer tuning at this scale.
🔬
Data volume compounds
Doubling training subjects (533 → 1,067) produced consistent improvement across all models. More data matters more than architecture refinements at this stage — a finding that generalises widely.
👁️
Explainability remains open
LIME setup ran but visualization was incomplete. GradCAM explored but not retained. Knowing a model is accurate is necessary but not sufficient — what it's actually looking at in the brain scan is the next question.
🔭
ViTs need pretraining on medical data
From-scratch ViT performance near random is not surprising — it's a data requirement problem. Pretrained ViTs (post-submission) demonstrated the gap can be closed substantially with the right initialization.

Reflection & Future Work

What I'd do differently — and what I'd do next

Known limitations

Slice-level train/test splits rather than subject-level — the more rigorous approach to avoid potential data leakage between slices from the same subject.

100,510 slices from 1,067 subjects is a reasonable scale for CNNs but insufficient for from-scratch Transformer models. Subject-level data is the true bottleneck, not slice count.

LIME and GradCAM explainability remain incomplete — accuracy without interpretability is not enough for clinical deployment. This is the most important open thread.

What comes next

GradCAM implementation — visualizing which brain regions activate for ASD classification is the natural next step. Identifying consistent activation patterns across subjects would be a meaningful clinical contribution.

Subject-level splits + cross-site validation — testing whether a model trained on data from one MRI scanner generalizes to scans from different machines is a critical practical requirement.

Multimodal fusion — combining sMRI (structural) with fMRI (functional connectivity) data, which the ABIDE dataset also provides. The combination may reveal patterns invisible in either modality alone.

3D convolutions — treating the full MRI volume as a 3D input rather than extracting 2D slices, preserving spatial relationships across the entire brain structure.

This project taught me something that no course ever could: that the gap between a model that works in a notebook and a system that a clinician would trust is enormous — and that closing that gap is exactly the kind of hard problem worth spending a career on.
