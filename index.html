B.Tech — Origin · 2023 · MIT Manipal · Prof. J. Andrew
ASD Detection via Neuroimaging
CNN vs ViT on ABIDE-I (1,067 subjects, 17 sites). CNN: 91% accuracy, AUC 0.98. ViT: 60% — data scale determines architecture viability. The lesson that shaped every decision after.
Medical imaging pipelines · NIfTI/DICOM · Architecture trade-offs at data scale
Needed: More data, real clinical datasets, proper segmentation, temporal modeling
MS Research · CSCE 6260 · Fall 2025 · Prof. Xiaohui Yuan
Fetal Head Segmentation — Cine-Loop
Real clinical dataset (HC18 ultrasound), proper segmentation (Residual U-Net), temporal modeling (3D U-Net). Built Pseudo-LDDM — synthesised video training data from still images when none existed. 86.2% Dice.
U-Net segmentation · 3D convolution · Synthetic data generation · Clinical measurement pipelines
Needed: Model efficiency for deployment, compression techniques
MS Research · CSCE 5934 Directed Study · Fall 2025 · Prof. Russel Pears
CNN Filter Pruning — Hybrid Crossover
Hybrid Crossover: synthesise a new filter from two redundant ones instead of deleting. Result: 2× compression, +0.37% accuracy, 2× faster runtime on VGG-16.
Model compression · Regression-based synthesis · Guard-rail engineering · Production-aware pruning
Needed: Multi-modal data, audio/signal processing, cloud-scale data engineering

What I'd contribute on day one — a realistic work scenario

Picture a mid-size radiology group integrating an AI triage tool across three hospital sites. The clinical team is receiving chest X-rays from different scanner models. The AI vendor's model is flagging too many false positives on one site because its scanner produces slightly different image characteristics. This is a real, recurring problem in clinical AI deployment.

Here's where my specific skills land: I understand DICOM-native data pipelines (from the ASD and Fetal Head work). I know how to audit model performance across data subsets with different distributions (from the histopathology scaling study). I've dealt with latency and hardware constraints in a deployment context (from CNN Pruning and WECARE). I've worked with clinical datasets including MIMIC-IV ICU data (from the AIWH course). I can write the evaluation code, identify the site-specific performance gap, and work with the clinical team to understand whether the issue is a preprocessing step, a calibration gap, or a retraining requirement — and communicate that clearly to both the engineering team and the radiologist.

This is the kind of problem I want to be solving from June 2026 — not in a lab, but in a working clinical environment. I'm a fresh graduate, but I've built every component of this scenario across the last two years.

Where I Want to Work

Ready for real clinical AI work

Not just research directions — concrete ways AI is being deployed in healthcare today, and where my skills fit directly into those workflows.

01
Radiology AI — Triage & Decision Support
AI is already in radiology departments — flagging critical findings, prioritising worklists, and reducing radiologist burden. I want to build the detection and segmentation pipelines behind those systems: training on DICOM-native data, validating across scanners, and deploying on hospital infrastructure rather than research servers.
02
Pathology AI — High-Throughput Screening
Digital pathology labs are replacing glass slides with whole-slide images at scale. The value is in the throughput — automated cancer screening across thousands of cases per day. The histopathology work in this portfolio was built precisely for this: understanding how data volume affects model reliability in a screening pipeline.
03
Clinical Deployment — From Model to Workflow
A model that works in a notebook is not a product. Getting AI into clinical use means handling DICOM pipelines, inference latency within radiologist workflow timings, integration with PACS systems, and passing clinical validation. The CNN pruning and WECARE work both addressed real deployment constraints — not just benchmark metrics.
04
Wearable & Remote Patient Monitoring
Hospitals are extending their reach beyond physical walls through wearables. The infrastructure for cardiac monitoring, fall detection, and chronic disease management is being built now. WECARE's on-device pipeline was built for exactly this gap: clinical-grade accuracy with no cloud dependency, for patients who cannot always be in a clinic.
05
Healthcare Operations & Clinical Intelligence
AI in healthcare isn't only about imaging — it's also about patient flow, resource allocation, and using clinical data (like MIMIC-IV) to improve outcomes at the system level. The Big Data and AIWH work gave me a foundation in the infrastructure and data pipelines that make clinical intelligence possible in practice.
06
Trustworthy AI for Clinical Teams
Clinicians need to understand and trust AI outputs before they act on them. Explainability isn't a research nicety — it's a regulatory and practical requirement. I've worked with Grad-CAM and LIME in real imaging contexts, and I understand that the goal is not a publishable heatmap but a visualisation a radiologist or pathologist can use to make a better decision faster.
